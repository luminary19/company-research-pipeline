# Synthesis: Harvey — Engineering Interview Prep & Reverse Due Diligence

> **The decision this answers** (Phase 0): you are interviewing for an **Engineering/Research** role at Harvey in ~1–3 weeks. This synthesis (a) equips you to walk in credible on the bet, product, market, and org, and (b) gives you an honest reverse-DD read on whether Harvey is a company worth joining. Tags: **F** fact, **I** inference, **A** assumption + confidence. Full evidence + citations live in the seven `dossier-*.md` files; this integrates them.

---

## 1. The one-paragraph verdict

Harvey is the **category-defining company in legal AI** — ~$11B valuation, ~$300M ARR (a first $100M+ net-new-ARR quarter, Jul 2026), 75% of the AmLaw 100, ~960 people, backed by the best investors alive [F/85%]. At the **engineering** level it is emphatically **not** a thin wrapper: a real multi-cloud agent runtime, production RAG with custom legal embeddings, an eval-data flywheel run by embedded ex-BigLaw lawyers, and serious security are all primary-documented systems [F/88%]. Its durable moat is **distribution + proprietary eval-data + services, not technology** — and the single decisive question, which even the CEO names aloud, is whether that moat compounds faster than the **foundation labs (OpenAI, Anthropic) now entering legal directly** commoditize the application layer [A/55%]. **For you as a candidate:** this is a place to do genuinely hard, greenfield AI-platform work with frontier-lab comp and elite peers, inside a high-intensity culture, at a company whose technology is real but whose long-term moat is a live, unresolved bet. Both halves are true; hold both.

## 2. The integrated picture — seven dossiers, seven tensions

Each dossier established a slice and handed forward one load-bearing tension. Together they are the through-line.

1. **Thesis** — The bet (vertical legal AI as "the operating system for legal") is real and directionally validated: legal is a ~$385B text-in/text-out market, adoption flipped skeptic→buyer, and Harvey's own LAB shows long-horizon legal work is <10% solved by frontier models — far from saturated, leaving room for a vertical player [F/90%]. **Tension:** the moat is *not* at the model layer (Harvey conceded its own model lost to frontier models on its own benchmark), so durability must come from workflow + eval-data + distribution — a bet, not a fact [A/55%].
2. **Company** — A genuinely large, fast-growing, founder-controlled entity: Seed→$11B in ~3.5 years, >$1.2B raised, Sequoia on the board (3 rounds led), ~$300M ARR, ~960 staff [F/85%]. **Tension:** every growth number is self-reported at a ~58× ARR multiple; the entity is real and well-capitalized (runway isn't the risk), but the *durability* of the reported growth is not externally verifiable.
3. **Product** — At the engineering level, not a wrapper: OpenAI-Agents-SDK "Tool Bundles," own multi-cloud runtime (Spectre), LanceDB + `voyage-law-2-harvey` RAG, on-demand vision, tenant-isolated security [F/90%]. **Tension:** the marketed autonomy overshoots the shipped reality — "agents execute end-to-end" is qualified by mandatory human-in-the-loop, several 2026 marquee surfaces are Early Access, and the own-model (GLM-5.1 post-train) is a benchmark result, not a shipped product.
4. **Buyers** — Prestige-first ICP (AmLaw-100 + Big-Four legal arms), expanding to corporate in-house (now ~41% of revenue), sold via a 6–9-month forward-deployed-engineer motion; jobs = high-volume pattern work, not advocacy/judgment [F/85%]. **Tension:** demand is genuine and prestige-validated, but capped by the billable-hour conflict, verification tax (ABA Op. 512), easy multi-vendor substitution, and an ROI that independent surveys say most in-house teams have *not* yet seen.
5. **Competitive** — Harvey leads a three-front war: incumbents own the case-law corpus it lacks (CoCounsel/Lexis/vLex), Legora is a genuine co-leader (~$5.6B), and — the existential front — the labs it *rents from* are entering legal directly (Anthropic made Harvey a connector inside Claude; OpenAI hired Ironclad's founder) [F/88%]. **Tension:** the moat is distribution+data+services, not technology; Weinberg himself calls the labs "our main competitor."
6. **Activity** — A relentless ship-and-raise sprint (4 raises in 12 months, a launch every few weeks), executing a three-pronged land-grab: own the model layer cheaply, own the agent/ecosystem layer, expand the market [F/88%]. **Tension:** it's a coherent, well-capitalized land-grab *into a market the labs are entering on a clock* — the next 6–12 months decide whether the moat entrenches or erodes.
7. **People** — An elite, domain-integrated, ~960-person org: a deep exec bench (incl. ex-Google legal leadership), and the signature bet of ~200+ embedded ex-lawyers ("Applied Legal Researchers") who *are* the eval moat [F/85%]. **Tension:** frontier-lab comp and smart peers, inside a high-intensity "re-earn-your-role" culture (Glassdoor WLB ~3.4), competing with the very labs it depends on for the same engineers.

## 3. The through-line: does the moat outrun the labs?

Every tension resolves to **one question**: Harvey is racing two compounding curves —

- **Curve A (Harvey's moat):** AmLaw-100 install base + firm data gravity + the lawyer-graded eval/data flywheel + forward-deployed distribution + enterprise security. This is real and growing [I/72%].
- **Curve B (commoditization):** frontier labs going vertical, offering the onboarding Harvey charges for, at a fraction of the price, while the model layer (where Harvey has no edge) keeps improving [F/85%].

The bull case: Curve A compounds (data gravity + relationships + eval flywheel are hard to replicate fast), Harvey stays the "operating system for legal," and the ~$300M ARR / 75%-AmLaw trajectory continues. The bear case: Curve B wins — the labs absorb the layer (the Feb-2026 ~$285B legal-tech market-cap wipeout and Freshfields "choosing neither" are early tremors), switching costs prove low (one-year deals, multi-vendor norm), and the ~58× multiple compresses. **This is genuinely unresolved from outside** [A/55%], and it is the single most important thing to have a *view* on walking into the interview.

## 4. Reverse due diligence — is Harvey worth joining (as an engineer)?

**Reasons it's a strong bet for a candidate:**
- **The engineering is real and hard** (product §3) — you'd build genuine AI-platform systems, not glue code, and the AI Platform team is explicitly "early/greenfield" [F/82%].
- **Runway is not the risk** — >$1.2B raised, ~$300M ARR, "not burning a crazy amount" (Weinberg); the company is durably capitalized [F/85%].
- **Comp is frontier-lab tier** (~$200–340K base + equity, ~$375K median total) [F/80%], with $11B-valuation upside.
- **You'd work with elite, domain-integrated peers** (ex-Google/DeepMind/Meta engineers + embedded ex-BigLaw lawyers) on the actual moat (evals/domain integration) [F/82%].
- **Category leadership** gives real distribution and problem access most startups can't offer.

**Honest risks to weigh:**
- **Moat durability is a live bet** (§3) — the labs are the existential threat, and even Harvey's CEO says so. Equity upside depends on Curve A winning.
- **Valuation risk** — ~58× ARR prices in near-perfection; a down-round/compression is a real scenario if growth decelerates or the labs win [I/60%].
- **Culture intensity** — "re-earn your role every 6 months," WLB ~3.4, in-office SF/NY 3+ days; smart but pressure-cooker [I/60%].
- **Talent war** — the labs (and Legora) compete for exactly your skills; retention pressure is real [I/55%].
- **The autonomy ceiling is a category truth** — legal agents aren't end-to-end yet (LAB <10%), so the "AI replaces lawyers" narrative is years out; you'd be building toward it, not shipping it [F/88%].

**Net:** a high-quality, high-intensity bet with strong comp and real engineering, whose long-term equity value hinges on an unresolved moat-vs-labs race. Reasonable to join with eyes open; the interview is your chance to pressure-test Curve A yourself.

## 5. The engineering interview-prep brief

### 5.1 The 90-second Harvey narrative (be able to say this cold)
"Harvey is domain-specific AI for legal + professional services — founded 2022 by Winston Weinberg (ex-O'Melveny lawyer) and Gabe Pereyra (ex-DeepMind), after a cold email to Sam Altman made the OpenAI Startup Fund their first investor. It's grown to ~$11B, ~$300M ARR, 75% of the AmLaw 100, ~960 people. The thesis: the durable value in legal AI is the *vertical layer* — matter-aware workflow, a proprietary lawyer-graded eval flywheel, forward-deployed distribution, and enterprise security — on top of multi-model frontier intelligence, not a single owned model. The big open question is whether that moat holds as OpenAI and Anthropic enter legal directly."

### 5.2 Know these numbers/facts cold
- Trajectory: Seed $5M (2022) → $11B (Mar 2026); ~$300M ARR; 75% AmLaw 100; ~960 staff (~21% ex-lawyers); ~142,000 users; 25,000+ custom agents.
- Architecture: **OpenAI Agents SDK + Tool Bundles + leave-one-out eval gates**; own multi-cloud (Azure+GCP) agent runtime (**Spectre**); multi-model routing; **LanceDB** RAG + **voyage-law-2-harvey** embeddings; on-demand vision; Temporal ingestion; tenant isolation at the DB layer; ZDR.
- Evals: **BigLaw Bench** + **LAB** (1,200+ tasks, 24 practice areas, 75k+ rubric criteria, all-pass grading; frontier <10% all-pass). Own model = post-trained **GLM-5.1** (rubric 0.853→0.913).
- Stack: Python (asyncio/FastAPI) + Go + React/TS/Tailwind; K8s, Terraform/Pulumi, Datadog/Sentry.

### 5.3 Likely interview questions + how to approach
- **System design (the differentiator):** "design a RAG pipeline over millions of legal docs per customer" → talk chunking, `voyage-law-2`-style domain embeddings, LanceDB/IVF-PQ, **tenant isolation at the store layer**, metadata filtering, reranking, citation grounding, freshness. "Design an eval harness for legal correctness" → **all-pass rubric grading** ("95%-right is wrong"), LLM-judge + grader alignment, leave-one-out gates, cost/latency budget. Reference Harvey's own blog approach back to them.
- **Coding:** medium DSA, often NLP/pipeline-framed (Python or TS). Standard prep + be fluent in both languages.
- **The "OpenAI-dependence / multi-model-risk" probe:** have a crisp view — Harvey's answer is own-the-runtime + multi-model routing + cheap post-trained models + differentiate on security/governance/collaboration/memory/integrations (thesis §3.4). Show you understand the moat-vs-labs tension without being naïve about it.
- **Behavioral:** ownership, customer empathy for lawyers, regulated-industry care (privilege, audit trails, malpractice/ABA 512).
- **Prep grounding:** clone and read **`github.com/harveyai/harvey-labs`** (the LAB harness + eval-strategy/architecture docs) and the Harvey engineering blog — speaking their own architecture fluently is the highest-leverage prep.

### 5.4 Sharp questions to ask them (signal you did the work)
- "How does the AI Platform team think about the moat-vs-labs question as you build — where does the legal-specific layer stay defensible when a frontier model can do 80% out of the box?"
- "How do Tool Bundle eval gates scale combinatorially as capabilities multiply — is that still the bottleneck the '3 Principles' post flagged?"
- "How is the GLM-5.1 post-trained model moving from benchmark into production, and what's the cost/latency budget a customer actually tolerates (the ~$50/task LAB number)?"
- "How do embedded Applied Legal Researchers and engineers actually share ownership day-to-day — 'merging English,' as the blog put it?"
- "What's the plan for the research-corpus gap (no native Westlaw/Lexis) — build, keep renting, or route around it?"

### 5.5 Three things NOT to get wrong in the room
- Don't call Harvey a "ChatGPT wrapper" — at the eng level it isn't (product §7); the wrapper critique is a *business-moat* argument, and knowing that distinction is itself a signal.
- Don't overstate agent autonomy — "end-to-end" is marketing; the shipped reality is review-ready drafts + human-in-the-loop (LAB <10% all-pass). Regulated-industry care is the value.
- Don't confuse the metrics — SPA 98.47% (task accuracy), LAB 7.1% (all-pass), rubric 91.3%, BigLaw Bench 74% are four different things.
