# Dossier, Buyers

## Owns (single source of truth)

This dossier owns **the demand side**: who buys Harvey (ICP segments), the buyer personas and decision unit, the jobs-to-be-done, the buyer journey/GTM motion as experienced by the customer, realistic market sizing (buyer-side TAM), pricing as willingness-to-pay, and the buyer-side adoption friction.

**Does NOT own:** whether the bet is durable (dossier-thesis); the entity/traction figures (dossier-company owns the customer counts and ARR; this dossier owns *why they buy*); what ships (dossier-product owns the product; this dossier owns the *job* it's hired for); the competitor briefs (dossier-competitive — this dossier owns the buyer's *multi-vendor behavior*, not the rival specs); the org/people (dossier-people). Named-customer *facts* are shared with dossier-company; the *reason and journey* are owned here.

## 1. The ICP — who actually buys

> Purpose: fix the ideal customer, core vs expansion.

**1.1 Prestige-first, top-down.** Harvey's core ICP is **elite large law firms (AmLaw-100, Magic Circle) and the Big-Four/professional-services legal arms** — "the AmLaw 100 and 4,000-lawyer Big Four legal arms, not the average 5-lawyer firm" [F/85%] (Ascero). Penetration rose from **42% of the AmLaw 100 (Aug 2025)** to "a majority" / ~50–60 firms (2026) [F/82% claimed] (Artificial Lawyer; harvey.ai/customers). The pattern set by A&O (2023) is the template: win a marquee firm, others follow.

**1.2 The expansion vector: corporate in-house.** Corporate/in-house legal is the fast-growing segment — corporate share of revenue went **4% → 33% → 41%** (early 2025 → Jan 2026) [F/85% claimed] (TechCrunch; TBPN). Named in-house buyers: HSBC, Deutsche Telekom, Repsol, Syngenta, Bayer, plus "500+ in-house teams" and "50 asset management firms" (company-reported). *The expansion is real but see §5 — pitching in-house directly can antagonize the law-firm channel.*

**1.3 Explicitly NOT for.** Mid-market and solo/small firms are priced out by design (§4); "small firms refused demos by design" [F/80%] (multiple pricing sources). Those segments go to Spellbook / CoCounsel Core / GC AI (dossier-competitive).

## 2. The decision unit & personas

> Purpose: who champions, approves, and uses — the "buyer-user gap."

**2.1 A federation, not a company.** A law firm is "a federation of partner-owned practices"; **the buyer and the user are different people** — a managing partner, GC, or innovation/KM lead signs, but associates, paralegals, and KM lawyers do the work, and "even the CIO cannot force a senior equity partner to adopt" [F/82%] (Perspective). This makes adoption a per-partner, per-practice-group sell, not a single enterprise decision.

**2.2 The champion personas (named).** Early adoption was champion-led by named "change agents": **David Wakeling** (A&O, ran the MIG pilot), **Bivek Sharma** (PwC UK Chief AI Officer), **Dr. Claudia Junker** (Deutsche Telekom GC — who set a "data stored in Germany" security precondition) [F/85%] (harvey.ai/customers/pwc-uk, /deutsche-telekom). The modern buyer added a role: a **Chief AI Officer / legal-ops / "Transformation Office"** counterpart inside the firm.

**2.3 Implication for the product.** Because the user (associate) and the buyer (partner) differ, and because the associate's *output* is what the partner bills, adoption hinges on trust + verification (§5). The security precondition (data residency, ethical walls) is frequently a gating requirement, not a nice-to-have.

## 4. The buyer journey & pricing (willingness-to-pay)

> Purpose: how the sale actually happens and what firms pay.

**4.1 The GTM motion.** **Lighthouse** design-partner co-development (A&O, PwC) → hyper-personalized demo → **paid pilot** (e.g., CMS ran a 12-month, 300-lawyer pilot) → parallel security/privilege review → **6–9-month forward-deployed-engineer** deployment across the firm → **land-and-expand** (seat-doubling) [F/82%] (Perspective). Harvey productized the change-management side as an official **"Transformation Office"** [F/80%] (harvey.ai). Sales cycle ~6 months; no self-serve.

**4.2 Pricing (heavily corroborated; no public pricing).** ~**$1,000–1,200/seat/month, 20-seat minimum, 12-month term → ~$288K/yr floor**; premium/Lexis-bundled seats higher (~$2,400/mo); mid-market deals $500K–$2M+ [F/85%, ~8 independent sources]. No free trial; demo-request is the only entry. This is the premium outlier vs incumbents (CoCounsel ~$225, Protégé ~$275, Bloomberg AI free-with-sub — dossier-competitive).

**4.3 The willingness-to-pay problem.** Firms justify the price via hours saved and the billable-hour economics (thesis §2.3). But the markup over raw model access is stark: one worked comparison put ~$630/mo (Claude API) vs ~$80,000/mo (Harvey) for 30k contract reviews — a 10–40× markup [I/60%, single-origin TCO, C3] (eesel). The premium buys the workflow, security, eval, and FDE deployment — the question §5 raises is whether that premium holds.

## 5. Buyer-side adoption friction (the skeptic section)

> Purpose: the demand-side risks, graded honestly.

**5.1 The billable-hour conflict.** Firms are "**reluctant to use their own lawyers to train a tool that could be used to replace them**" [F/85%] (Bloomberg Law). And the ROI is contested: an **ACC survey found ~60% of in-house counsel saw no cost reduction, and only 13% saw fewer billable hours** [F/80%] (JD Journal); an "8am" report found **83% of clients aren't even pushing for AI price cuts** [F/75%]. Firms "may simply bill traditionally while using AI internally to increase margins" — which is good for Harvey's stickiness but undercuts the "AI transforms legal economics" story [I/70%].

**5.2 The verification burden (structural).** **ABA Formal Opinion 512** treats AI output as requiring full lawyer verification (AI = a nonlawyer assistant under Rule 5.3; you can't bill the AI-saved time) [F/85%]; **Mata v. Avianca** and later sanctions make the malpractice risk concrete. Every hour of "saved" work carries a verification tax — a real cap on the productivity claim.

**5.3 Easy substitution / multi-vendor norm.** Firms run a stack, not a monopoly — "Harvey for the thinking, Kira for the reviewing, CoCounsel for the citing" [F/78%] (AI Vortex). CONFLICT surfaced: some report **one-year deals** that make the lead fragile (Bloomberg Law) while others report **contract lengths *lengthening*** (Law.com, May 2026) — carried, not resolved. Net: switching cost is contested, not clearly high.

**5.4 Channel risk.** Harvey (and Legora) "pitching directly to corporate legal departments" poses "a potential problem for law firms" [F/75%] (Bloomberg Law) — the in-house expansion (§1.2) can cannibalize the law-firm relationship, and in-house teams are also *insourcing* (ACC: 64%). The innovator's-dilemma "copilot → autopilot" trap is a live risk.

## 6. Buyer-side TAM (analytical spine)

> Purpose: the realistic market, with the sizing softness surfaced.

- **Total legal services:** US **~$385.6B (2025) → $439.2B (2030)** [F/80%] (Research and Markets); global **~$1.05T (2024)** [F/78%] (Grand View). Consistent with Sequoia's 2023 "$300B+ US legal" framing (thesis §2.2) at an earlier date.
- **Legal tech:** **→ ~$50B by 2027** (from ~$23B in 2022), per Gartner via Bloomberg Law [F/70%, single-origin].
- **Legal-AI *software* (the serviceable slice):** estimates **diverge ~3×** for the same year — $3.1B→$10.8B (MarketsandMarkets), $1.5B→$8.3B (SNS Insider), $5.2B→$40.9B (AgentMarketCap). **No defensible point TAM** — the divergence is itself the finding [F/75%].
- **Sequoia's own framing:** a "$60B autopilot" opportunity, of which only ~$20–25B is in the essay's prose (the rest inferred from a chart), and the essay doubles as a portfolio disclosure (Sequoia co-led Harvey's $200M round) [I/65%] — treat backer TAM as interested.
- **Serviceable reality for Harvey:** AmLaw-100 + Magic Circle + Big-Four legal + large in-house, at ~$1k+/seat — a premium slice of the top of the market, not the whole $385B. The expansion thesis (tax/finance/compliance/professional services) is what makes the TAM look large, and it is unproven (thesis A-list).

## Open Questions / Decisions Pending

- **Is the ROI real at the firm level?** The productivity claims are firm-disclosed and impressive, but independent surveys show most in-house teams saw no cost reduction. The billable-hour conflict is unresolved and load-bearing for durable demand.
- **CONFLICT carried:** one-year deals (fragile) vs contract-lengthening (sticky) — the switching-cost question that decides the lock-in debate (also thesis §6.5).
- **Sizing softness:** legal-AI-software TAM diverges ~3×; no defensible point figure. Any "huge TAM" claim rests on the professional-services expansion, which is a bet, not a fact.
- **Load-bearing tension handed to synthesis:** demand is genuine, prestige-validated, and expanding to in-house — but capped by the billable-hour conflict, the verification tax, easy multi-vendor substitution, and a ~$288K floor that excludes the market's long tail. For an engineering candidate: the buyers are real and demanding (BigLaw partners), which is *why* the eval/verification/security engineering matters so much — the product must be trustworthy enough to survive a malpractice-averse buyer.
- **Routed this session:** competitive (multi-vendor stack behavior; Kira/CoCounsel/Harvey split; channel-conflict); activity (Transformation Office launch; in-house expansion as strategy); people (Chief-AI-Officer buyer persona → who Harvey hires to sell/deploy, FDE motion); thesis (ROI-not-proven survey data strengthens §6.5–6.6).
