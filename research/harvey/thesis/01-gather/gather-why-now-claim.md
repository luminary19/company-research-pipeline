## Findings

### Sub-question 1 — Why-now forces

**1a. The "a-ha moment" was GPT-3 in late 2021, validated by a blind attorney test.** Pereyra showed Weinberg OpenAI's GPT-3 "at the end of 2021"; Weinberg used it on a landlord-tenant pro-bono case at O'Melveny, they built a chain-of-thought prompt over California landlord-tenant statutes, ran it on 100 r/legaladvice questions, and gave the answers to three landlord-tenant attorneys blind. "Of 100 questions, 86 said yes" [they would send the answer]. This is repeated near-verbatim across FT, TechCrunch, Observer, Forbes and the OpenAI case study — but all trace to Harvey's own retelling. [R2·C3] (Source: FT interview, 2026-05-20, <https://www.ft.com/content/47eafdcb-5a0a-4515-a07a-70e89ad2fa78>); corroborating tellings [R2·C3] (TechCrunch, 2025-11-14, <https://techcrunch.com/2025/11/14/inside-harvey-how-a-first-year-legal-associate-built-one-of-silicon-valleys-hottest-startups/>) and [R1·C3] (OpenAI case study, <https://openai.com/index/harvey/>).

**1b. GPT-4 was the pivotal unlock that changed the bet from a niche tool to "this is going to change big law."** Weinberg (FT): "We pitched them to build something particularly for landlord-tenant disputes. That pitch changed when we got access to GPT4, because it doesn't take the same amount of time to come up with the prompt and evaluate the prompt and evaluate the output as it does to do the work. [And we thought] well, this is going to change big law." Sequoia: "Last year, Harvey became the first company built on top of GPT-4." Pereyra (Forbes): "Our partnership with OpenAI gave us very early access to GPT-4—almost a year before the rest of the world saw it. And we used that time to really understand how to make these models work for the law." [R2·C2] (Sources: FT 2026-05-20, <https://www.ft.com/content/47eafdcb-5a0a-4515-a07a-70e89ad2fa78>; Sequoia "Partnering with Harvey," 2023-04-26, <https://sequoiacap.com/article/partnering-with-harvey-putting-llms-to-work/>; Forbes Australia, 2026-03-12, <https://www.forbes.com.au/news/innovation/what-is-harvey-ai-the-legal-tech-decacorn-changing-how-lawyers-work/>).

**1c. Why law specifically: chain-of-thought reasoning maps onto how law works.** Weinberg (FT): "If you think about what law is, it's apply statutes and then the interpretation of those statutes to a fact pattern. That is perfect for chain of thought reasoning. Part of it was the chain of thought prompting works super well in legal, and it doesn't work in a lot of other areas." [R2·C3] (single origin Weinberg) (Source: FT 2026-05-20, <https://www.ft.com/content/47eafdcb-5a0a-4515-a07a-70e89ad2fa78>).

**1d. Harvey's own official why-now framing (2023): a shift in the delivery/consumption of professional services.** Harvey Series A post (Pereyra & Weinberg): "GPT-4 marks a shift in how professional services will be provided and consumed. Large language models have the potential to give knowledge workers superpowers by solving complex tasks efficiently and accurately." [R1·C3] (Harvey's own claim) (Source: Harvey blog, 2023-04-26, <https://www.harvey.ai/blog/sequoia-and-openai-back-harvey-to-redefine-professional-services-starting-with-le>).

**1e. The market/economics of the knowledge work being targeted: "text-in, text-out," $300B+ US legal market, "rote cognitive labor."** Sequoia (Pat Grady & Charlie Curnin): "Harvey is starting with the legal market, which is $300Bn+ in the U.S. alone. Legal work is the ultimate text-in, text-out business—a bull's-eye for language models." They frame the founders as understanding "both the problem (rote cognitive labor) and the solution (large language models)." [R2·C3] ($300B figure single origin Sequoia; framing is a backer's) (Source: Sequoia 2023-04-26, <https://sequoiacap.com/article/partnering-with-harvey-putting-llms-to-work/>).

**1f. Billable-hour / fee-structure pressure is a core why-now force.** Weinberg (FT): "I don't think the billable hour is going away, I think what's going to happen is people will pay fixed fees for tasks — in a large M&A [transaction] they'll do fixed fees for diligence… the way they price this value needs to change." He argues the old model is illogical: "it was billed as if a bunch of junior associates did all these research tasks at $1,000 an hour, and then the partner… billed me for like 5 hours, at $3,000 an hour. That actually doesn't make sense." Also: "What's going to happen is in-house teams are going to force law firms to change… you're not going to accept crazy bills from a law firm to do that particular task." Pereyra (Forbes) frames dismantling the billable hour as a "significant, if unintended, consequence," pushing firms toward "value-based or fixed-fee models." [R2·C2] (Sources: FT 2026-05-20, <https://www.ft.com/content/47eafdcb-5a0a-4515-a07a-70e89ad2fa78>; Forbes Australia 2026-03-12, <https://www.forbes.com.au/news/innovation/what-is-harvey-ai-the-legal-tech-decacorn-changing-how-lawyers-work/>).

**1g. Law firms shifted from AI-skeptic to AI-adopting; early traction came from a handful of "change agents."** Weinberg (Observer): "the industry as a whole has changed profoundly in a very short time with regard to attitudes towards A.I." Early adoption was about "finding the futurists, like David Wakeling at A&O Shearman, Claudia Junker at Deutsche Telekom and Bivek Sharma at PwC," and "We also got a lot of 'nos.'" [R2·C2] (Source: Observer, 2026-03-18, <https://observer.com/2026/03/harvey-ceo-winston-weinberg-ai-law/>).

**1h. The stated progression: productivity tool → enterprise system → agentic infrastructure.** Weinberg (FT): "these products are moving from a productivity tool for an individual user to a system for an enterprise… A lot of these products are going to transform from individual productivity to something you sell to an institution. And the institution basically is like a bunch of agents… which work with the humans." Harvey FORUM thesis (official): "AI in legal is moving beyond experimentation and becoming infrastructure, and the next phase will be about acceleration"; "what started as tools for isolated drafting or research are evolving into systems that reshape how legal and professional services are delivered." GIC/Sequoia press release (Weinberg): "AI isn't just assisting lawyers. It's becoming the system through which legal work gets done." [R1·C1] (multiple independent + Harvey official) (Sources: FT 2026-05-20, <https://www.ft.com/content/47eafdcb-5a0a-4515-a07a-70e89ad2fa78>; Harvey "The Year Legal AI Becomes Core Infrastructure," 2026-03-16, <https://www.harvey.ai/blog/the-year-legal-ai-becomes-core-infrastructure>; GIC/Sequoia press release, 2026-03-25, <https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2026/03/Harvey-Raises-at-11-Billion-Valuation-from-GIC-and-Sequoia-to-Scale-Agents-Across-Law-Firms-and-Enterprises.pdf>).

### Sub-question 2 — Harvey's stated core claim / vision over time

**2a. 2022 founding claim: "a natural language interface to the law."** Pereyra (quoted via TechCrunch in LawNext, Nov 2022): "We want Harvey to serve as an intermediary between tech and lawyer, as a natural language interface to the law. Harvey will make lawyers more efficient… Harvey provides a unified and intuitive interface for all legal workflows, allowing lawyers to describe tasks in plain English instead of using a suite of complex and specialized tools for niche tasks." And: "Our goal is not to compete with existing legal tech (such as Westlaw or LexisNexis) or to replace lawyers… We see a future where every lawyer is a partner — able to delegate the busy work of the legal profession to Harvey." [R1·C2] (primary founder quote) (Source: LawNext, 2022-11-23, <https://www.lawnext.com/2022/11/stealth-legal-ai-startup-harvey-raises-5m-in-round-led-by-openai.html>).

**2b. 2023 vision — the constant thread: "not a chatbot that compliments your workflow; it will be the platform your workflow is built on."** Harvey Series A post (Pereyra & Weinberg): "At Harvey, we believe that the future of AI will not be a chatbot that compliments your workflow; it will be the platform your workflow is built on. This is why we partner with entire firms… You can't build self-driving cars in a lab and understanding the real problems faced by our clients is fundamental to making those problems AI-soluble." [R1·C3] (Harvey's own) (Source: Harvey blog, 2023-04-26, <https://www.harvey.ai/blog/sequoia-and-openai-back-harvey-to-redefine-professional-services-starting-with-le>).

**2c. 2024–2025: "domain-specialized AI," explicitly positioned against being a thin wrapper.** Harvey o1 post (Sep 2024): frames its work as delivering "on our promise of domain-specialized AI"; "closing this domain-specific reasoning gap is exactly what the Harvey team has spent two years solving"; foundation models like o1 "outperform existing foundation models, though fall short… compared to Harvey's domain-specialized assistant models." On the "ChatGPT wrapper" criticism, Weinberg (TechCrunch, 2025): "for 2023 and 2024, a lot of the power behind the product is honestly the model, plus front-end work… But if you're trying to build something where I have 100,000 documents in this data room, 5,000 emails about this M&A, all these different statutes and codes, and I want… a system where I can ask questions over all of those pieces combined with high accuracy — that's the holy grail." He names two moats: workflow-data / evaluation, and being "very strongly multiplayer." [R1·C1] (Sources: Harvey o1 blog, 2024-09-12, <https://www.harvey.ai/blog/harvey-building-legal-agents-and-workflows-with-openai-s-o1>; TechCrunch 2025-11-14, <https://techcrunch.com/2025/11/14/inside-harvey-how-a-first-year-legal-associate-built-one-of-silicon-valleys-hottest-startups/>).

**2d. 2026 vision: "essential infrastructure" / "operating system for legal" / "the platform on which legal work runs" + agentic + memory.** LawNext podcast summary (Jan 2026): the founders describe evolving "from building an AI assistant for individual lawyers to constructing what they call 'essential infrastructure' for legal work," and a "vision for becoming an AI operating system that integrates across the entire legal tech ecosystem," with a "focus on memory and agentic AI that can handle complex multi-step workflows." GIC/Sequoia press release (Mar 2026): Harvey described as "the operating system for legal and professional services"; Grady: "Harvey has become the platform on which legal work runs." Memory feature co-built with industry, per Pereyra (Forbes): "Instead of each interaction starting from scratch, Memory allows the platform to retain context from previous work." [R1·C1] (Sources: LawNext, 2026-01-20, <https://www.lawnext.com/2026/01/lawnext-from-roommates-to-billionaires-harveys-founders-gabriel-pereyra-and-winston-weinberg-on-building-ai-infrastructure-for-law.html>; GIC/Sequoia press release, 2026-03-25, <https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2026/03/Harvey-Raises-at-11-Billion-Valuation-from-GIC-and-Sequoia-to-Scale-Agents-Across-Law-Firms-and-Enterprises.pdf>; Forbes Australia, 2026-03-12, <https://www.forbes.com.au/news/innovation/what-is-harvey-ai-the-legal-tech-decacorn-changing-how-lawyers-work/>).

**2e. KEY: the vision "stayed the same, just sped up."** Weinberg (FT), asked how the original thesis developed: "We've actually kept the same vision, it's just sped up. We thought that basically what we would have to do is be building an assistant for a solo lawyer for a very long time. And then eventually you could aggregate that process into a platform for a firm or an in-house team. And the thing that's happening really fast is I think these products are moving from a productivity tool for an individual user to a system for an enterprise." [R2·C2] (Source: FT 2026-05-20, <https://www.ft.com/content/47eafdcb-5a0a-4515-a07a-70e89ad2fa78>).

**2f. Pereyra's early strategic bet: build a general-purpose platform, not a narrow workflow tool — and "infrastructure over models."** Notion "First Block" episode summary (Feb 2026): "Harvey's early instinct was to build a general-purpose legal AI rather than starting with one specific workflow like NDA review. This mirrors what happened in coding: The winning product wasn't a tool for one language or one task, it was a comprehensive AI associate capable of handling anything." Episode chapters include "Building a General Tool" and "Infrastructure Over Models." [R3·C2] (podcast-summary paraphrase, not a verbatim quote) (Source: Notion blog, 2026-02-18, <https://www.notion.com/en-gb/blog/first-block-with-gabe-pereyra>).

### Sub-question 3 — The domain-specific vertical bet (beats horizontal/general models)

**3a. Sequoia's framing (2023): Harvey as "the AI super app," starting with lawyers then all of knowledge work.** Pat Grady & Charlie Curnin: "Harvey is the first and best instantiation of a new breed of company: the AI super app. Harvey is in the business of giving people superpowers. First, lawyers. Next, professional services. Eventually, all of knowledge work." [R2·C3] (backer's own thesis, single origin) (Source: Sequoia 2023-04-26, <https://sequoiacap.com/article/partnering-with-harvey-putting-llms-to-work/>).

**3b. OpenAI case study: general foundation models "lacked the knowledge required for legal work," so Harvey built a custom-trained case-law model.** "Foundation models were strong at reasoning, but lacked the knowledge required for legal work. So, Harvey decided to partner with OpenAI to build a custom-trained model." They trained on Delaware case law then all U.S. case law, "the equivalent of 10 billion tokens." Result vs. GPT-4: "97% of the time, the lawyers preferred the output from the case law model." Pereyra's stated design principle: "Don't build for the current capabilities of models today—build for where the models are going to be." [R1·C3] (single origin Harvey/OpenAI; 97% and 10B-token figures cap at C3) (Source: OpenAI case study, <https://openai.com/index/harvey/>; corroborated in CEO-NA, <https://ceo-na.com/technology/harvey-partners-with-openai-to-build-a-custom-trained-model-for-legal-professionals/> and LawSites 2024-05-01, <https://www.lawnext.com/2024/05/harvey-ai-to-move-out-of-early-access-phase-release-more-affordable-versions-of-its-custom-ai-models.html>).

**3c. Harvey's own benchmark (BigLaw Bench) is used to argue proprietary/domain models beat foundation models.** "Harvey's proprietary models outperform leading foundation models on domain-specific tasks, producing 74% of a final, expert lawyer-quality work product — the outputs are more detailed, capture more nuance, and are much closer to final lawyer quality." [R1·C3] (Harvey's own benchmark, self-reported) (Source: Harvey "Introducing BigLaw Bench," <https://www.harvey.ai/blog/introducing-biglaw-bench>).

**3d. Weinberg's explicit vertical thesis: eval frameworks per vertical get "crazy specific" and generic lab benchmarks become "useless."** FT: "this is a reason why vertical companies… will exist: the eval frameworks for each vertical are going to get crazy, crazy, crazy specific. The benchmarks that the AI labs release and how they do eval is getting completely useless for vertical companies. What we need is eval on fund formation for this type of fund, with this amount of LPs, by document type in these 70 jurisdictions." Also: "The way that you build each of these agents is different for every single practice area… jurisdiction, then… practice area, then… what happened in this particular case." [R2·C2] (Source: FT 2026-05-20, <https://www.ft.com/content/47eafdcb-5a0a-4515-a07a-70e89ad2fa78>).

**3e. Harvey's own stated positioning of the bet's chief risk: the model labs are "the most likely competitors in vertical A.I."** Weinberg (Observer): "long-term, the model companies are the most likely competitors in vertical A.I. That doesn't change our approach, but it does increase our urgency to focus on differentiated components, like security, governance, collaboration, memory and integrations." (Recorded here as Harvey's own framing of its vertical thesis, not as the skeptic case, which another slice owns.) [R2·C3] (Source: Observer 2026-03-18, <https://observer.com/2026/03/harvey-ceo-winston-weinberg-ai-law/>).

**3f. "Multi-model by design" and owning the runtime are cast as pillars of the vertical/application-layer bet.** Harvey (Niko Grupen, Mar 2026): "We built a multi-model platform because doing so is the only responsible way to serve organizations whose work demands the best available intelligence, uninterrupted continuity, and full control… quality, reliability, and choice." On owning agent infrastructure: "Owning the runtime is also what makes the legal-specific layer possible at all… None of that is something a general-purpose runtime will solve for the legal industry, and all of it is what makes owning this layer durable rather than temporary." Weinberg (FT) generalizes: "every single application layer company is going to have their own models… you don't want to have a single provider." [R1·C2] (Sources: Harvey "Why Harvey is Multi-Model by Design," 2026-03-10, <https://www.harvey.ai/blog/why-harvey-is-multi-model-by-design>; Harvey "Why we Built our own Cloud Agent Infrastructure," <https://www.harvey.ai/blog/why-we-built-our-own-cloud-agent-infrastructure>; FT 2026-05-20, <https://www.ft.com/content/47eafdcb-5a0a-4515-a07a-70e89ad2fa78>).

**3g. June 2026: Harvey announces a "legal foundation model series" — the vertical bet extended to owning legal-specific models.** Pereyra (via Law.com, reporting his X post): Harvey is building "the first legal-specific AI model in its new legal foundation model series," working "as an agentic system that controls legal tech tools and relies on help from frontier AI models, such as OpenAI's GPT-5." Two stated goals: "1. Allow us to serve frontier intelligence across our product surface areas at an affordable price and a strong security posture. 2. Create the foundations for law firms to build their own specialized models and own their own intelligence." Training data/benchmarks to be open-sourced; research team is Harvey Labs (run by ex-Google Brain researcher Niko Grupen and ex-O'Melveny associate Julio Pereyra). [R2·C2] (Source: Law.com, 2026-06-18, <https://www.law.com/legaltechnews/2026/06/18/harvey-announces-development-of-custom-legal-specific-ai-models-/>).

**3h. Pat Grady's "AI-native application company / Salesforce cloud transition" framing (secondary, primary origin unlocated).** Grady is quoted in the GIC/Sequoia press release (primary): "Harvey has become the platform on which legal work runs… we believe it's positioned to become one of the most important companies of the next decade." [R1·C3]. Two secondary trade blogs additionally attribute to Grady: "They wrote the playbook for what it means to be an AI-native application company, which is the same thing Salesforce did back in the day with the cloud transition." The Salesforce phrasing was NOT found in a primary Sequoia/Harvey source in this pass. [R3·C3] (Sources: GIC/Sequoia press release, 2026-03-25, <https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2026/03/Harvey-Raises-at-11-Billion-Valuation-from-GIC-and-Sequoia-to-Scale-Agents-Across-Law-Firms-and-Enterprises.pdf>; AI Agent Corps, 2026-03-27, <https://www.agentcorps.co/blog/vertical-ai-agents-harvey-ai-11-billion-valuation>; Jugger Insight, 2026-03-29, <https://juggerinsight.com/en/harvey-ai-legal-agent-11b-valuation-2026-en/>).
